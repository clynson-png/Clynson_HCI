# HCI HAPPY LIBRARY CANONICAL V1

## Purpose
Canonical exercise library for the HAPPY Prescription Engine. The library maps HAPPY/COMANDO gaps to athlete-coach developmental exercises.

## Engine Flow
HAPPY INPUT -> DIMENSION ENGINE -> GAP DETECTION -> CRITICAL PARAMETER -> HAPPY LIBRARY -> PRESCRIPTION -> DEVELOPMENT PLAN -> REASSESSMENT

## Selection Policy
- Primary selection: lowest dimension score.
- Secondary selection: lowest weighted parameter inside the dimension.
- Tie-breakers: lowest parameter value, largest perception gap, coach priority, competition proximity.
- The dashboard displays. The engine decides. The library prescribes.

## Severity Bands
| Score | Status | Prescription Load |
|---|---|---|
| 1.0-1.99 | CRITICAL | 3 exercises, coach follow-up required |
| 2.0-2.99 | ATTENTION | 2 exercises, review next session |
| 3.0-3.99 | STABLE | 1 maintenance exercise |
| 4.0-4.49 | HIGH_PERFORMANCE | reinforcement only |
| 4.5-5.0 | ELITE | protect and monitor |

## Canonical Exercise Entries
| Parameter | Name | Objective | How to do |
|---|---|---|---|
| CONFIDENCE | Performance Evidence Journal | Build confidence from real performance evidence, not from motivation alone. | After each training session, the athlete records 3 objective evidences of capability: a good series, a corrected mistake, a stable routine, or a pressure moment handled well. Review before key sessions. |
| CONFIDENCE | Success Replay | Reactivate a proven competitive state. | The athlete selects one successful match, series, or technical block and mentally replays posture, breathing, focus, rhythm, and decision quality for 3-5 minutes before training. |
| CONFIDENCE | Confidence Anchor | Create a quick trigger for competitive confidence. | Recall a strong performance. At the emotional peak, use a consistent gesture and cue word. Repeat 5 times. Use the same anchor before pressure blocks. |
| ORGANIZATION | Training Week Map | Organize the week around the athlete objective. | At the start of the week, athlete and coach define training days, recovery, technical focus, mental focus, and one non-negotiable habit. Athlete confirms daily completion. |
| ORGANIZATION | Pre-Session Checklist | Reduce friction before practice. | Before training, athlete checks equipment, hydration, arrival time, training objective, warm-up, and data recording method. Missing items are marked for coach follow-up. |
| ORGANIZATION | Routine Audit | Find routine breakdowns that affect consistency. | Once per week, compare planned training with actual training. Identify missed sessions, late starts, sleep problems, or equipment/logistics issues. |
| MANAGEMENT | Execution Contract | Convert the plan into visible execution. | Before training, athlete writes: today I will execute X, measure Y, and adjust Z. After training, mark done/partial/not done and explain one reason. |
| MANAGEMENT | Plan-to-Action Review | Check whether coach planning is becoming athlete execution. | At the end of the session, athlete reports what was planned, what was executed, what was skipped, and what needs adjustment. |
| MANAGEMENT | Daily Process Score | Prioritize process over result. | Athlete scores process execution from 1 to 5 after training and writes one behavior to repeat next session. |
| ANALYSIS | After Action Review | Transform training into learning. | After training, answer: What was the objective? What happened? Why did it happen? What will I repeat? What will I change? |
| ANALYSIS | Error-to-Data Reframe | Prevent mistakes from becoming identity. | When a bad shot or poor series appears, athlete writes it as data: trigger, rhythm, posture, arousal, focus, or decision. Then chooses one correction. |
| ANALYSIS | Learning Log | Track recurring lessons. | At the end of the week, athlete lists the 3 most repeated lessons and coach assigns one technical or mental priority for the next block. |
| NERVE | Pressure Reset Routine | Recover after error under pressure. | Use one breath, one posture reset, one cue word, and one process instruction before the next shot/block. Train it after intentional pressure simulations. |
| NERVE | Pressure Simulation Ladder | Build tolerance to competitive pressure. | Coach creates blocks with increasing constraints: score target, time limit, public counting, consequence, or final-shot scenario. Athlete must keep process routine stable. |
| NERVE | Emotional Control Window | Identify when pressure enters the sequence. | After each block, athlete marks if pressure appeared before the first shot, during execution, after an error, or at the end. Coach links it to intervention. |
| DISCIPLINE | Non-Negotiable Habit | Strengthen consistency through one daily action. | Choose one small daily behavior connected to the objective. Track completion for 14 days. Missed days require a short cause note, not punishment. |
| DISCIPLINE | Minimum Session Rule | Protect continuity on low-motivation days. | When the athlete cannot complete full training, execute a minimum version: 10 minutes dry-fire, 10 technical reps, or one mental routine. Keep the chain alive. |
| DISCIPLINE | Commitment Review | Reconnect behavior to declared objective. | Once per week, compare objective, completed sessions, missed actions, and recovery discipline. Athlete chooses one correction for the next week. |
| OPPORTUNITY | Feedback Utilization Log | Ensure coach feedback becomes action. | After receiving feedback, athlete writes the instruction, the next drill where it will be applied, and the evidence that will show improvement. |
| OPPORTUNITY | Opportunity Scan | Recognize growth chances in the environment. | Weekly, athlete lists available opportunities: coach feedback, competition, sparring, data review, video, equipment check, or recovery. Select one to use. |
| OPPORTUNITY | Coach Question Practice | Increase active learning. | Before coach review, athlete prepares one specific question about technique, pressure, routine, or execution. Coach answers and assigns an action. |
| HEED | Arousal Awareness Log | Improve awareness of competitive arousal before, during, and after performance. | Athlete rates arousal 1-5 before training, mid-session, and after. Add one note: too low, optimal, or too high. Compare with rhythm and pressure data. |
| HEED | Optimal Competitive Arousal Routine | Reach the athlete optimal arousal zone. | Before training or competition, athlete adjusts breathing, posture, visual focus, internal dialogue, and attentional cue. Then rates readiness from 1 to 5. |
| HEED | Pre/Post Check-In | Connect internal state to performance evidence. | Answer quickly before and after training: arousal, focus, confidence, body tension, and perceived readiness. Coach compares with actual performance indicators. |
| ALIGNMENT | Coach-Athlete Alignment Session | Clarify what the athlete must do to approach the objective. | Once per week, coach and athlete define the main objective, the reason behind it, the key action, and how success will be measured. |
| ALIGNMENT | Training Direction Card | Convert strategy into a simple athlete instruction. | For each session, coach provides one sentence: today the priority is __ because __. Athlete repeats it in their own words before starting. |
| ALIGNMENT | Misalignment Flag | Detect when the athlete does not understand the plan. | If ALIGNMENT <= 2 or PLAN <= 2, athlete must write what is unclear. Coach resolves with one technical objective and one measurable criterion. |
| PLAN | Plan Fit Review | Check whether the coach plan fits the athlete reality. | At the end of the week, athlete rates plan fit 1-5 and identifies barriers: time, fatigue, clarity, equipment, pressure, or recovery. Coach adjusts if needed. |
| PLAN | Plan Adherence Sheet | Measure adherence without blame. | For each planned session, mark completed, partial, missed, or modified. Add one reason and one next action. |
| PLAN | Plan Confidence Check | Detect resistance to the process. | Athlete answers: Do I understand the plan? Do I believe it fits the goal? Can I execute it this week? Scores below 3 trigger coach conversation. |
| POWERING | Energy Investment Score | Measure how much energy the athlete is applying to the plan. | Before and after training, athlete rates energy investment 1-5. Coach compares with physical degradation, rhythm, and pressure indicators. |
| POWERING | Activation Block | Raise energy without losing precision. | Use a short warm-up sequence: posture, breathing, mobility, visualization, and one technical dry-run. Finish when arousal is ready but not excessive. |
| POWERING | Effort Quality Review | Separate effort from useful effort. | After training, athlete answers: Was my energy directed to the correct task? Did I rush, force, or stay precise? Choose one adjustment. |
| YEARN | Goal Contract | Convert long-term desire into visible commitment. | Athlete writes the season objective, why it matters, what must be sacrificed, and the weekly minimum behaviors required. Coach reviews monthly. |
| YEARN | Time Audit | Compare ambition with actual dedicated time. | For 7 days, athlete records time spent on training, recovery, analysis, physical preparation, and distraction. Compare with stated goal. |
| YEARN | Champion Mindset Statement | Make the objective emotionally clear. | Athlete writes a short statement: what I am pursuing, why I want it, what I will do when motivation is low, and what I will stop doing. |
